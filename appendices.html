Appendices: Mathematical Preliminaries, Reading Path & Worked Examples

This page collects three reference tools rather than narrative chapters: Appendix A gives the minimum notation and mathematics assumed throughout the site, restated in learner-friendly form; Appendix B is an ordered, annotated path through the primary papers behind the book's claims — read in this order rather than the order they were published; Appendix D works through the exact arithmetic behind several numbers quoted informally elsewhere on the site, so you can reproduce (and sanity-check) them yourself.

Appendix A. Mathematical Preliminaries

Reference

Source: LLM Architecture Evolution, pp. 62–64.

Why this mattered

Every chapter on this site uses a small, fixed set of mathematical tools — chain-rule probability, cross-entropy, softmax, and gradient/Jacobian reasoning about deep networks. This appendix gathers them in one place with plain-language explanations, so a chapter can use a term like "KL decomposition" or "Jacobian product" without re-deriving it, and so you have one page to return to if a chapter's math moves faster than you'd like.

Notation

Chain-rule factorization & cross-entropy

Every autoregressive language model factors the joint probability of a whole sequence into a product of next-token conditionals, exactly, with no independence assumption:

P(x₁,…,x_T) = P(x₁) · P(x₂ | x₁) · P(x₃ | x₁,x₂) ⋯ = ∏t=1T P(xₜ | x<ₜ)

This is an identity from basic probability (repeated application of the definition of conditional probability), true regardless of what computes each conditional — an n-gram count table, an RNN's hidden state, or a Transformer's attention output. Training minimizes the cross-entropy (equivalently, negative log-likelihood) between the model's predicted distribution and the actual next token:

L = − (1/T) ∑t=1T log Pθ(xₜ | x<ₜ)

Cross-entropy decomposes as the true distribution's own entropy plus the KL divergence from the model's distribution to the true one — since the true distribution's entropy is fixed and cannot be reduced by training, minimizing cross-entropy is exactly equivalent to minimizing that KL divergence, i.e., making the model's predictions as close as possible to the true conditional distribution. Perplexity is simply exp(cross-entropy), rescaling the loss into "effective number of equally-likely choices" units that are easier to reason about intuitively than a raw log-loss number.

Softmax, temperature & sampling

Softmax converts a vector of real-valued scores ("logits") into a probability distribution:

softmax(z)ᵢ = exp(zᵢ) / ∑ⱼ exp(zⱼ)

Temperature τ divides the logits before softmax (softmax(z/τ)): τ < 1 sharpens the distribution toward the highest-scoring tokens (more deterministic, less diverse output); τ > 1 flattens it (more diverse, more error-prone output). Top-k sampling restricts sampling to only the k highest-probability tokens before renormalizing; top-p (nucleus) sampling instead keeps the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p, adapting the candidate set size to how confident the distribution is at each step (a peaked distribution keeps very few tokens; a flat one keeps many).

Gradients, Jacobians & the residual argument

Backpropagation through a chain of layers multiplies each layer's local Jacobian (the matrix of partial derivatives of its output with respect to its input) together via the chain rule. If each Jacobian has eigenvalues consistently below 1, the product shrinks toward zero as more layers are chained — vanishing gradients; if consistently above 1, the product grows unboundedly — exploding gradients. A residual connection x → x + f(x) has Jacobian I + J_f (identity plus the local Jacobian of f) — because the identity component is always exactly 1 regardless of depth, chaining many residual layers multiplies matrices that are each close to identity, which is structurally far more resistant to vanishing/exploding than chaining arbitrary Jacobians directly, independent of what f computes.

Complexity notation

This site uses big-O notation to describe how a quantity scales with sequence length n or another size parameter, not to give an exact operation count. "O(n²)" means the quantity grows roughly proportionally to n squared for large n — doubling n roughly quadruples the quantity — regardless of the constant multiplying it. This is why, for example, self-attention's O(n²) training cost and a state-space model's O(n) cost describe fundamentally different scaling behavior, even though for any one specific, small n the actual wall-clock numbers depend on implementation details this notation deliberately ignores.

Going deeper: the full KL decomposition of cross-entropy

For true distribution P and model distribution Q, cross-entropy H(P, Q) = −∑ P(x) log Q(x) decomposes exactly as H(P, Q) = H(P) + D_KL(P ‖ Q), where H(P) is the true distribution's own entropy (irreducible — it does not depend on the model at all) and D_KL(P ‖ Q) = ∑ P(x) log(P(x)/Q(x)) is the Kullback–Leibler divergence, which is always non-negative and zero only when Q = P exactly. Because H(P) is a constant with respect to the model's parameters, minimizing cross-entropy over training is mathematically identical to minimizing D_KL(P ‖ Q) — this is the precise sense in which "cross-entropy training" means "make the model's distribution match the true one."

Checkpoint

  1. Is the chain-rule factorization of sequence probability an assumption or an identity?
    Reveal answerAn identity — it follows directly from the definition of conditional probability and holds for any joint distribution, regardless of what mechanism computes each conditional term.
  2. What happens to the sampling distribution as temperature τ increases past 1?
    Reveal answerThe distribution flattens — probability mass spreads more evenly across tokens, increasing output diversity but also the chance of selecting a low-probability, likely lower-quality token.
  3. Why does a residual connection's Jacobian (I + J_f) resist vanishing/exploding gradients better than an arbitrary layer's Jacobian?
    Reveal answerBecause its identity component is always exactly 1 regardless of depth, so chaining many residual layers multiplies matrices that stay close to identity, rather than multiplying arbitrary Jacobians whose eigenvalues could compound multiplicatively away from 1 across many layers.

Appendix B. Annotated Primary-Source Reading Path

Reference

Source: LLM Architecture Evolution Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66, reorganized as a reading path and supplemented with dated external sources.

Why this mattered

A plain alphabetical bibliography tells you what exists but not what order to read it in. This appendix reorders the book's cited works (plus a small number of research-added papers) into a five-stage reading path, each stage annotated with why it matters and what to look for — cite these primary papers, not this monograph, when you need a source for a specific claim.

Stage 1 — Attention & the Transformer

  1. Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio, "Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate" (2014). See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66. Read first: introduces additive attention as a fix for the seq2seq encoder bottleneck (Ch. 7–8) — the direct ancestor of everything that follows in this stage.
  2. Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need" (2017). arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762. The Transformer itself (Ch. 9) — read for the self-attention formula, multi-head attention, and the original (post-norm) block layout.
  3. Foundational LayerNorm work, cited as the normalization Chapter 9's original block used before RMSNorm (Ch. 13). See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66.

Stage 2 — Scaling & the modern block

  1. Radford et al., "Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training" (GPT-1) (2018). cdn.openai.com — GPT-1 paper. Establishes decoder-only causal pretraining plus fine-tuning (Ch. 10).
  2. Devlin et al., "BERT" (2019). arxiv.org/abs/1810.04805. Read alongside GPT-1 for the encoder-only, masked-language-modeling contrast (Ch. 11).
  3. Kaplan et al., "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" (2020). arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361. First rigorous power-law treatment of loss vs. parameters/data/compute (Ch. 12).
  4. Hoffmann et al., "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models" (Chinchilla) (2022). arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556. Read directly after Kaplan — revises the compute-optimal parameter/data split (Ch. 12; worked in Appendix D.3 below).
  5. Zhang & Sennrich, "RMSNorm" (2019). arxiv.org/abs/1910.07467; Shazeer, "GLU Variants Improve Transformer" (SwiGLU) (2020). arxiv.org/abs/2002.05202; Su et al., "RoFormer" (RoPE) (2021). arxiv.org/abs/2104.09864. Read these three together — they are the core "modern block" ingredients of Chapter 13.
  6. Shazeer, "Fast Transformer Decoding" (MQA) (2019). arxiv.org/abs/1911.02150; Ainslie et al., "GQA" (2023). arxiv.org/abs/2305.13245. Read after the modern-block trio for the KV-cache-compression spectrum (Ch. 13).

Stage 3 — Efficiency: MoE & long context

  1. Fedus, Zoph & Shazeer, "Switch Transformers" (2021). arxiv.org/abs/2101.03961. Start here for MoE — the simplest top-1 routing design (Ch. 14).
  2. Mistral AI, "Mixtral of Experts" (2024). arxiv.org/abs/2401.04088; Dai et al., "DeepSeekMoE" (2024). arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066. Read next, in this order, for top-2 routing and then fine-grained shared+routed experts.
  3. Dao et al., "FlashAttention" (2022). arxiv.org/abs/2205.14135. Read for the online softmax/tiling mechanism (Ch. 15; worked in Appendix D.4 below) before its sequels.
  4. Kwon et al., "PagedAttention" (2023). See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66. Read after FlashAttention — a serving-time cache-management technique, distinct in purpose from FlashAttention's training/compute-time tiling.
  5. Peng et al., "YaRN" (2023). arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071. Read last in this stage for post-hoc context-extension arithmetic (Ch. 15; Appendix D.6 below).

Stage 4 — Alternatives & frontiers

  1. Gu & Dao, "Mamba" (2023). arxiv.org/abs/2312.00752. Read for the selective state-space mechanism and parallel scan (Ch. 16); read the underlying S4/HiPPO papers cited within it if you want the full derivation.
  2. Liu et al., "Lost in the Middle" (2023). See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66. Read before or alongside Chapter 17 — directly informs where to place retrieved passages in a RAG pipeline.
  3. Dosovitskiy et al., "Vision Transformer" (2020). arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929; Radford et al., "CLIP" (2021). arxiv.org/abs/2103.00020. Read together for the standard visual-encoder foundation used across Chapter 18's fusion patterns.
  4. Alayrac et al., "Flamingo" (2022). arxiv.org/abs/2204.14198; Liu et al., "LLaVA" (2023). arxiv.org/abs/2304.08485. Read after ViT/CLIP to contrast cross-attention injection with token-concatenation fusion (Ch. 18).

Stage 5 — Reasoning & test-time compute

  1. Wei et al., chain-of-thought prompting. See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66. Read first — establishes the "scratch tape" framing central to Chapter 19.
  2. Lightman et al., process reward model training. See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66. Read next for the PRM-vs-ORM distinction.
  3. Snell et al., inference-time compute scaling. See PDF Appendix B bibliography, pp. 64–66. Read for the test-time scaling framing that unifies best-of-N, self-consistency, and search.
  4. OpenAI, "Learning to reason with LLMs" / o1 system card (2024). openai.com/index/learning-to-reason-with-llms, arxiv.org/abs/2412.16720; DeepSeek-AI, "DeepSeek-R1" (2025). arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948. Read last, and treat as historical snapshots to re-verify — this stage moves fastest of the five.

How to use this path

Each stage assumes the previous one; within a stage, read in the listed order. This path intentionally does not match publication date order (e.g., Bahdanau et al. 2014 precedes Vaswani et al. 2017 for a reason: attention had to exist before the Transformer could remove recurrence around it) — it matches the order concepts are needed to understand the next paper.

Going deeper: why cite the papers instead of this website or the source PDF

This site and the source monograph are both secondary explanations, written to teach the material rather than to serve as citable evidence of a research claim. If you need to support a specific factual or numerical claim (a reported speedup, a parameter count, a benchmark score), cite the primary paper or technical report itself — check its publication date and version, since many of these figures (especially in Stages 3 and 5) are specific to one release and change across versions.

Checkpoint

  1. Why does this reading path put Bahdanau et al. (2014) before Vaswani et al. (2017) even though both predate later stages?
    Reveal answerBecause attention (Bahdanau et al.) had to exist as a concept before the Transformer (Vaswani et al.) could remove recurrence and rely on attention alone — the ordering follows conceptual dependency, not just publication date.
  2. Why should you cite Kaplan et al. or Hoffmann et al. directly, rather than citing this website, for a scaling-law claim?
    Reveal answerBecause this site is a secondary, teaching-oriented explanation of their findings, not the original evidence — citing the primary paper lets a reader verify the exact claim, its conditions, and its date.
  3. Which reading-path stage does the appendix warn moves fastest and should be treated most cautiously as a "snapshot"?
    Reveal answerStage 5 — Reasoning & test-time compute, covering o1/o3/DeepSeek-R1-style systems.

Appendix D. Worked Numerical Examples

Reference

Source: LLM Architecture Evolution, pp. 67–70. Figures below are rounded, illustrative arithmetic tied to specific stated assumptions — not general benchmarks.

Why this mattered

Chapters 12–16 make claims like "compute scales as roughly 6ND" or "GQA reduces KV cache size" without necessarily walking through the arithmetic. This appendix works six concrete examples end to end, in a given → find → solution format, so you can reproduce each number yourself and see exactly which assumptions it depends on.

Read this before the examples

Every number below depends on the stated assumptions (model size, hardware, precision, sequence length). Changing any assumption changes the result. Treat these as worked methods you can re-run with your own numbers, not as fixed facts about "how much LLMs cost."

D.1 Training compute (the 6ND rule)

Given

A dense Transformer with N = 70 billion parameters, trained on D = 2 trillion tokens (illustrative round numbers in the ballpark of a LLaMA-3-70B-class model).

Find

Approximate total training compute C, in floating-point operations (FLOPs).

Solution

The widely used approximation for dense-Transformer training compute is C ≈ 6ND — the factor of 6 approximates 2 FLOPs per parameter per token for the forward pass and 4 FLOPs per parameter per token for the backward pass (roughly twice the forward cost), summed. Substituting:

C ≈ 6 × (70 × 10⁹) × (2 × 10¹²) = 6 × 1.4 × 10²³ ≈ 8.4 × 10²³ FLOPs

This is a first-order approximation: it ignores attention's superlinear cost at very long sequence lengths, embedding/output-layer FLOPs, and implementation-specific overhead — treat it as an order-of-magnitude planning number, not an exact accounting.

D.2 KV-cache bytes

Given

A model with 80 layers, model dimension d = 8192, 8 KV heads under GQA (each of dimension 128, so 8 × 128 = 1024 total KV dimension per layer), a context length of 8192 tokens, batch size 1, and 16-bit (2-byte) precision for cached values.

Find

Total KV-cache memory for one full-context request.

Solution

Per token, per layer, the cache stores one key vector and one value vector across all KV heads: 2 (K and V) × 1024 (total KV dim) × 2 bytes = 4096 bytes. Across 80 layers: 4096 × 80 = 327,680 bytes per token. Across a full 8192-token context:

327,680 bytes/token × 8192 tokens ≈ 2.68 × 10⁹ bytes ≈ 2.68 GB

This is for one request at full context; multiply by concurrent-request batch size for total serving memory, and note that GQA's 8 shared KV heads (rather than, say, 64 heads under full MHA) already reduced this number by roughly 8× relative to a same-sized MHA model.

D.3 Chinchilla token allocation

Given

A fixed training compute budget C, and the Chinchilla-era fitted rule of thumb of roughly 20 training tokens per parameter as a compute-optimal allocation for the objective and data distribution Hoffmann et al. studied.

Find

An illustrative compute-optimal parameter count N and token count D for C ≈ 8.4 × 10²³ FLOPs (the budget computed in D.1).

Solution

Using D ≈ 20N and C ≈ 6ND: substitute to get C ≈ 6N(20N) = 120N², so N ≈ √(C / 120).

N ≈ √(8.4 × 10²³ / 120) ≈ √(7.0 × 10²¹) ≈ 8.4 × 10¹⁰ ≈ 84 billion parameters

Then D ≈ 20 × 84 × 10⁹ ≈ 1.68 × 10¹² ≈ 1.68 trillion tokens. Compare this to D.1's actual 70B/2T configuration: the same compute budget, allocated by the fitted 20-tokens-per-parameter rule, suggests a somewhat larger, somewhat-less-trained model than the illustrative 70B/2T pair — which is exactly the kind of allocation question Chinchilla's fitted law is meant to inform, not dictate exactly, since the "20" is itself a fitted constant specific to Hoffmann et al.'s experimental setup.

D.4 FlashAttention memory traffic

Given

A naive attention implementation for one attention head over a sequence of length n = 8192, storing the full score matrix in HBM at 2 bytes per entry (16-bit), versus a tiled FlashAttention implementation processing tiles of size 128 that fit entirely in on-chip SRAM.

Find

The size of the score matrix a naive implementation must write to and read from HBM, to see why avoiding that traffic matters.

Solution

The full score matrix has n × n = 8192 × 8192 ≈ 6.7 × 10⁷ entries; at 2 bytes each, that is:

8192 × 8192 × 2 bytes ≈ 1.34 × 10⁸ bytes ≈ 134 MB

— for a single attention head, at a single layer. A naive implementation writes this matrix to slow HBM and reads it back (at least) twice (once for the softmax normalization pass, once for the weighted value sum), while FlashAttention keeps each small 128×128 tile (128 × 128 × 2 bytes ≈ 32 KB) entirely within fast on-chip SRAM and never writes the full matrix to HBM at all — the saved traffic scales with the full matrix size shown above, multiplied by however many heads and layers the model has, which is why the saving compounds quickly at real model scale.

D.5 MoE active vs. total parameters

Given

An illustrative MoE layer with 256 routed experts of equal size, top-8 token-choice routing, plus 1 always-on shared expert of the same size as each routed expert (loosely modeled on the DeepSeekMoE/DeepSeek-V3 design pattern, with simplified round numbers for clarity, not exact DeepSeek-V3 figures).

Find

The ratio of active experts to total experts for one token, and what that implies about active vs. total feed-forward compute.

Solution

Total experts available: 256 routed + 1 shared = 257. Active experts for any one token: 8 routed (top-8) + 1 shared (always on) = 9.

Active fraction ≈ 9 / 257 ≈ 3.5%

Because each expert is the same size, feed-forward compute per token is also approximately 3.5% of what it would be if every expert ran for every token — this is the concrete arithmetic behind the qualitative claim in Chapter 14 that MoE total capacity can far exceed active per-token compute. (DeepSeek-V3's actual reported 671B-total/37B-active split, cited in Chapter 14, uses different, non-uniform expert sizing and counts — use this simplified example to build intuition for the ratio, and Chapter 14's cited report for that model's real figures.)

D.6 RoPE extension arithmetic

Given

A model trained with RoPE at a maximum context length of 4096 tokens, which you want to run at up to 16,384 tokens using simple linear position interpolation.

Find

The scaling factor applied to position indices before computing RoPE's rotation angles.

Solution

Linear position interpolation rescales every position index by the ratio of trained to target length before it is used in RoPE's rotation formula, so the model only ever "sees" rotation angles within the range it was trained on:

scale = trained_length / target_length = 4096 / 16384 = 0.25

A token at raw position 16,000 is treated, for rotation-angle purposes, as if it were at position 16,000 × 0.25 = 4,000 — within the original 4096-token trained range. This keeps rotation angles in-distribution (avoiding the worse degradation of naive extrapolation), but compresses relative-position resolution by the same 4× factor, which is why NTK-aware scaling and YaRN (Chapter 15) instead apply uneven scaling across RoPE's frequency components rather than one uniform factor, trading a more complex formula for better-preserved short-range precision.

Going deeper: sensitivity of these numbers to precision and hardware

Every byte-counting example above (D.2, D.4) assumes 16-bit (2-byte) storage; switching to 8-bit (FP8/INT8) storage roughly halves those figures, and switching to 32-bit roughly doubles them — precision is a first-order lever on memory-traffic and cache-size arithmetic, independent of any architectural choice. Similarly, the compute examples (D.1, D.3) treat FLOPs as a hardware-agnostic accounting unit; actual wall-clock training time additionally depends on a specific accelerator's achievable FLOPs/second, which is a separate, hardware- and kernel-specific number not computed here.

Checkpoint

  1. In the 6ND rule, why is the constant 6 rather than 2?
    Reveal answerBecause it approximates 2 FLOPs per parameter per token for the forward pass plus roughly 4 FLOPs per parameter per token for the backward pass (about twice the forward cost), summing to approximately 6.
  2. In D.2, what specifically caused the KV cache to be roughly 8× smaller than a same-sized full multi-head-attention model would produce?
    Reveal answerGQA sharing 8 KV heads across all query heads, rather than giving every query head (potentially 64 of them) its own KV head as full MHA would.
  3. Why does D.6's linear interpolation compress relative-position resolution, and what technique addresses that weakness?
    Reveal answerBecause it applies the same scaling factor to every position uniformly, shrinking the effective distance between any two positions by that factor; NTK-aware scaling and YaRN address this by applying uneven scaling across RoPE's different frequency components instead of one uniform factor.
